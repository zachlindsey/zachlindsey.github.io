A quick crash course in Fourier analysis: Suppose $f$ is a "nice" function, so that this exists: $$\hat{f}(\omega) = \int_t f(t) e^{-it\omega} dt$$ for all $\omega$. Then $\hat{f}$ is the Fourier transform of $f$, and if $f$ is especially nice then $$f(x) = \int_\omega \hat{f}(\omega) e^{it\omega} d\omega.$$ So we have this way to go back and forth $$f \Leftrightarrow \hat{f}.$$ A nice example: if $f(t) = 1_{[-1,1]}$ is a rectangle function, then $$\hat{f}(\omega) = \int_{-1}^1 e^{-it\omega}dt = \frac{i}{\omega} \bigg(e^{-i\omega} - e^{i\omega}\bigg) = \frac{2\sin(\omega)}{\omega}$$ is the sinc function. And we can compute the inverse: $$ \int_\omega \frac{2\sin(\omega)}{\omega} e^{i\omega t}d\omega = \int_\omega \frac{2\sin(\omega)}{\omega} \bigg( \cos(\omega t) + i \sin(\omega t) \bigg)$$

Suppose $x(t) = \sum_k c_k e^{i\lambda_k t}$ is a periodic time series we're interested in. (In particular, the sum is over a finite set of $n$.) One way we could try to figure out properties about it is to take the autocorrelation $$C(\tau) = \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{2T} \int_{-T}^T \overline{x(t)} x(\tau + t) dt.$$ We can calculate $$ \begin{align*} & \int_{-T}^T \overline{x(t)} x(\tau + t) dt \\ = & \sum_n \sum_m \overline{c_n} c_m \int_{-T}^T e^{it(\lambda_m - \lambda_n) + i\lambda_m \tau} dt \\ = & \sum_n \sum_m \overline{c_n} c_m e^{i\lambda_m \tau} \int_{-T}^T e^{it(\lambda_m - \lambda_n)}dt \\ = & 2T \sum_n |c_n|^2 e^{i\lambda_n \tau} + \sum_{n \neq m} \overline{c_n} c_m \frac{2\sin(T(\lambda_m - \lambda_n))}{\lambda_m - \lambda_n} \\ \end{align*} $$ Now we see why we must divide by $2T$ and take the limit, which gives $$C(\tau) = \sum_n |c_n|^2 e^{i\lambda_n \tau}.$$ Why do this? We seemingly just took something with frequencies $\lambda_k$ and made a new thing with the same frequencies! The point is that $C(-\tau) = C(\tau)$ or equivalent, the coefficients in its Fourier representation are real! So we have taken the complex numbers $c_k \in \mathbb{C}$ and converted them to reals $|c_k|^2 \in \mathbb{R}$. This is equivalent to tossing out the phase information and retaining only power information.
